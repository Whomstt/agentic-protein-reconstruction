\section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Proteins, Peptides and Proteomics}
Proteins are made up of chains of amino acids, often formed from smaller peptides with the complete set
expressed by an organism considered as its proteome \cite{https://doi.org/10.1002/imt2.70031}. The sequence
in which amino acids occur in a protein captures enough information to determine its structure and biological function
\cite{Dinh2026}. Proteins are often seen as the "language of life", capable of 
carrying out instructions that are found within our DNA, serving as enzymes for biochemical
reactions and providing the necessary support for cellular life structures \cite{https://doi.org/10.1002/imt2.70031}.
All of this complexity and diversity in functionality is captured within an alphabet consisting of 20 standard amino acids, motivating
the development of sequence models inspired by Natural Language Processing (NLP) approaches \cite{Dinh2026}.

In proteomics, the analysis of proteins is typically not completed on their full form, but rather through a bottom-up
approach involving an enzymatic digestion causing a decomposition of proteins into framgented peptides \cite{https://doi.org/10.1002/imt2.70031}.
The trypsin enzyme is often used for this process due its specificity in terms of splitting (cleaving)
proteins at terminal sites of lysine (K) and arginine (R) amino acids (residues) respectively \cite{https://doi.org/10.1002/imt2.70031}.
As the proteins under analysis can only be observed from a second-hand point of view through its
cleaved peptide fragments, bioinformatics software must be used to infer a mapping to the most likely
parent candidates \cite{https://doi.org/10.1002/imt2.70031}.

Enzymatic digestions are not perfect and often result in "missed cleavages"; when the protein
is not cut at the site it should have been; leaving us with longer sequences which can make
matching more difficult \cite{MCDONNELL20232732}. A significant portion of peptides observed in
a sample remain unidentified, in fact on average as little as 25\% of spectra obtained through
experimentation can be identified, while the rest is discarded. \cite{MCDONNELL20232732}. It is
common in proteomics for there to be an ambiguous mapping between peptides and proteins as they
are not unique, making it considerably difficult to classify which parent protein the candidate peptide
belongs to \cite{MCDONNELL20232732}. This further motivates a need for more advanced reconstruction
algorithms, capable of mapping fragments from noisy signals to their original form, especially when
considering under-documented or undiscovered proteomes that often occurs during pure culture experimentation.

\subsection{Protein Language Models}
Advances in NLP through Large Language Models (LLMs) have enabled a 
better appreciation of the "language of life" \cite{9477085}
through training on large datasets of proteins. Protein Language Models (pLMs) transfer the notion of using
tokens to represent amino acids in a self-supervised process, effectively learning to predict
masked amino acids from known sequences instead of words \cite{9477085}.

While the training objective remains simple in nature, when performed over millions of proteins
the model begins to internalise more than just sequence patterns, but also the evolutionary structure
\cite{lin2023esmfold}. Training an additional folding head on top of the base pLM allows us to transfer
knowledge from cheaply learned internal representations before making expensive 3D structure predictions
\cite{lin2023esmfold}.

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) have been almost entirely replaced by the transformer architecture
in the field of protein-sequence modeling since ProtBert \cite{9477085} highlighted the benefits 
of self-attention in capturing long range dependencies of proteins on a large-scale. Our structural understanding
of proteins has been enabled through models like ESMFold \cite{lin2023esmfold} which allows for
prediction of 3d structures directly from single sequences.

Despite these major advances, pLMs remain limited in their functionality. While folding models like ESMFold are
exceptionally more efficient at structural predictions than their alignment-based counterparts; performing up
60x faster in inference time compared with the likes of AlphaFold and RosettaFold \cite{lin2023esmfold}, these models
focus on predicting structures from known sequences rather than designing or inferring novel ones. Moreover,
current pLMs are fundamentally restricted by our limited viewpoint from data on known existing proteins 
which have already been produced by nature, inhibiting us from potentially uncovering proteins which differ
from these captured patterns.

\subsection{De Novo Protein Identification}
Identifying proteins is a non-trivial task, we typically perform a bottom-up strategy where 
peptides are matched using a database with an average 75\% of spectra obtained
through experimentation remaining unidentified \cite{MCDONNELL20232732}. During an enzymatic
digestion such as a trypsin digestion, proteins are decomposed into peptide fragments which
break at certain points called cleavage sites. Peptides do not always fragment as expected, 
or aren't fully cleaved during enzymatic digestion. This poses the greatest challenge in 
peptide identification when fragments are incomplete or ambiguous \cite{MCDONNELL20232732}.

Machine learning has been successful in recovering structural information from incomplete signals
on several accounts in proteomics. Traditional CNNs such as DeepNovo only look at local neighbours
within a spectrum, where hybrid CNN-GNN models allow for long-range interconnections \cite{MCDONNELL20232732};
demonstrating that new architectures which combine existing ideas in new ways
are always being introduced and improving results.

Uncertainties in protein identification do not only appear on the spectrum level but also often on the sequence level.
In real world experimentation, only partial views of proteins and their respective peptide chains
are visible due to noise or errors occuring. Masked language modeling helps to simulate and train
a network to complete peptides with noise through randomly masking residues or single amino acids
and inferring their proper values through the surrounding context \cite{kharkar2024llmproteins}.
Fine-tuning transformer models like BERT and RoBERTa that were originally trained purely on natural language are capable
of adapting to the task of predicting missing amino acids, while pLMs including ProteinBERT
which have already been trained on millions of protein sequences can be fine-tuned further
to improve their performance on identifying specific species \cite{kharkar2024llmproteins}.

\subsection{De Novo Protein Design}
De novo protein design involves creating proteins from scratch rather than making changes to existing ones
that occur naturally. While creating untargeted de novo proteins is an interesting and useful exercise,
we consider the design of de novo proteins that match desirable structural makeups or 
useful properties as a much more challenging, and important task \cite{doi:10.1126/sciadv.adl4000}.
Proteins that occur naturally represent only a fraction of the theoretical potential number of possible
sequences; A peptide of length $L$ offers approximately $20^L$ possible sequences, most of which 
have never been observed in nature \cite{xia2025apexgensimultaneousdesignpeptide}.

ForceGen takes an interesting approach to design through mapping desired mechanical properties such as
toughness and strength rather than structural properties \cite{doi:10.1126/sciadv.adl4000}. Through a
combination of a frozen pretrained pLM (ESM-2) and a diffusion model developed to operate within the pLM's
probability latent space, this protein language diffusion model (pLDM) architecture was capable of creating
novel proteins through mapping a dataset of only 7,026 curated proteins \cite{doi:10.1126/sciadv.adl4000}.
As a measure of novelty, a BLAST validation was done to observe whether there was a significant similarity
to known proteins from the ones generated; they found many of the generated sequences did not match any
sequences in the database of known proteins \cite{doi:10.1126/sciadv.adl4000}.

A newer alternative to diffusion-based generative models is that of flow matching models. Flow matching
is supposed to bridge the gap between stochastic diffusion and domains which require more deterministic
outcomes \cite{xia2025apexgensimultaneousdesignpeptide}. ApexGen makes use a receptor-conditioned,
manifold-aware conditional flow matching framework capable of ensuring that the 3D structures of
generated de novo sequences will properly map to target binding sites \cite{xia2025apexgensimultaneousdesignpeptide}.
A template modeling (TM) score of 0.99 against the baseline comparison of PepFlow with 0.33 indicates ApexGen's
ability to generate near-perfect folds and a better global topology alignment \cite{xia2025apexgensimultaneousdesignpeptide}.

\subsection{Sequence Assembly Algorithms}
The problem of reconstructing original protein sequences from their fragmented peptides requires the use of
sophisticated sequence assembly algorithms to be done effectively. Standard approaches to proteomic assembly
make use of software which will match against known proteins from a database. Alternative approaches
often involve overlap-layout-consensus (OLC) and string graph approaches, spectral-network-based methods
and de Brujin graphs. The alternative graph-based approaches which do not rely on existing knowledge of
proteins existing is much more suitable for our problem.

A string graph is a representation of all that is inferable about a sequence from a collection of fragmented
readings collected from it \cite{10.1093/bioinformatics/bti1114}. String graphs are capable of removing
redundant edges from graphs without loss of sequence information, furthermore, we can filter out "contained
reads"; a reduction of roughly 40\% of reads in assembly data and 64\% of overlaps providing a significant
reduction in the size and complexity of the problem \cite{10.1093/bioinformatics/bti1114}. This graph
approach allows for a collapse of all reptitive elements into singular edges and ensures that the final
graph is "read coherent"; or a viable biological sequence formation \cite{10.1093/bioinformatics/bti1114}.

MS/MS database tools (e.g. SEQUEST, Mascot, and modern alternatives) often face computational
limitations when handling massive datasets of proteins, but where they struggle especially today is when it comes to 
the task of identifying unknown or modified proteins without a reference \cite{doi:10.1073/pnas.0701130104}.
Spectral networks bypass the use of databases completely through alignment of "spectral pairs"
(overlapping pairs of peptides or modified versions of the same) to form a full "spectral network" \cite{doi:10.1073/pnas.0701130104}.
Analysing the entire "spectral network" at once was shown to increase the confidence of peptide identifications
considerably, opening the door for shotgun protein sequencing \cite{doi:10.1073/pnas.0701130104}.


\subsection{Agentic AI in Proteomics}
Agentic AI has seen great success in recent times, particularly in the field of programming with tools
such as Claude Code completely changing the way that we build software. Traditional rule-based pipelines struggle with handling noisy feedback signals and
when things don't go as expected. An agentic system is controlled by an LLM "brain", which is granted access to "tools". Most agentic pipelines follow the ReAct pattern
where an LLM receives a prompt and "reacts" by executing the most relevant tools respective to the query
and its instruction. Sometimes tasks are so complex that it makes sense to have multiple agents acting together for a shared
objective. 

ProtAgents proposed a multi-agent system for de novo protein design which involved multiple
agents collaborating autonomously. A "Planner" agent develops a plan to solve the task, an "Assistant"
executes APIs and custom functions to compute the relevant data required to proceed, and a "Critic" is
responsible for giving feedback about the plan and analysing results produced by tools 
\cite{ghafarollahi2024protagentsproteindiscoverylarge}. Integration of diverse tools such as
OmegaFold and Chroma, physics-based simulations, and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) from
relevant scientfic literature provide direct feedback signals to the GPT-4 powered agents, 
allowing them to cooperate and design novel proteins with minimal human intervention \cite{ghafarollahi2024protagentsproteindiscoverylarge}.
In several experiments the "Critic" agent was able to correct JSON formatting errors from resulting tool
calls and plan accordingly, something which would have needed to be explicitly handled in a sequential
end-to-end pipeline \cite{ghafarollahi2024protagentsproteindiscoverylarge}.

VibeGen follows in the footsteps of ProtAgents, making use of multiple agents to generate de novo proteins,
but instead makes use of pLDMs \cite{ni2025agenticendtoendnovoprotein}.
Proteins designed by the system are created by targeted vibrational properties where the "Protein Designer"
agent generates sequence candidates given the target properties and the "Protein Predictor" evaluates these
candidates from their normal mode shapes without requiring expensive physics simulations \cite{ni2025agenticendtoendnovoprotein}.
While VibeGen is not agentic in terms of LLMs, it proves that even a relatively simple two-agent system produces 
considerably better outcomes than a single actor. The authors even suggest that LLM-powered behaviour could
enhance predictive power and mechanistic interpretability, further incentivising more work to be done in
this field \cite{ni2025agenticendtoendnovoprotein}.

\subsection{Comparative Analysis}
While pLMs such as ProtBERT \cite{9477085} and ESMFold \cite{lin2023esmfold} are excellent models
for understanding the sequences and structures of proteins, they rely on the assumption that we are
dealing with a protein which is complete and perfectly ordered; this is not enough alone for us
to unpack a mixture of peptide fragments and piece them back together to reconstruct the original
protein.

Masked language modeling has demonstrated success in predicting missing amino acids from surrounding
context using Transformer-based models \cite{kharkar2024llmproteins}, this means we can effectively
make use of a junction scoring mechanism to find plausible pairings of peptides, guiding the reconstruction
process.

Generative implementations like ApexGen \cite{xia2025apexgensimultaneousdesignpeptide} and
ForceGen \cite{doi:10.1126/sciadv.adl4000} are effective systems capable of designing de novo
proteins, however, they are not directed at reconstructing fragmented observed proteins which are
novel.

The application of agentic and multi-agent pipelines has shown great success for ProtAgents \cite{ghafarollahi2024protagentsproteindiscoverylarge}
and VibeGen \cite{ni2025agenticendtoendnovoprotein}, highlighting the power of granting agents
access to "tools" and having multiple agents cooperating to design targeted de novo proteins. VibeGen
particularly praises the capacity of an "LLM brain" in terms of coordinating the design of a novel
protein from scratch \cite{ni2025agenticendtoendnovoprotein}; while not at once relating to
our reassembly problem, its success implicitly indicates usefulness of agentic AI in other regions
of proteomics.

\subsection{Research Gap}
pLMs are effective at filling in gaps in unified protein sequences, however they have not previously
been used as a method to determine how fragments might join together.

Graph-based assembly approaches including spectral \cite{doi:10.1073/pnas.0701130104} and 
string graphs \cite{10.1093/bioinformatics/bti1114} are capable at reducing the
search space of potential fragment pairings that are feasible significantly without a database, but they should be paired
with AI models which understand the structural and sequential order of amino acids and peptides
which form a biologically plausible protein.

Agentic AI has worked well on several fronts already in the field of proteomics, usage of
tools providing relevant feedback and multiple agents working together have allowed for a more
hands-off approach in designing de novo proteins from scratch. We have not however previously
seen its usage in reconstruction of observed proteins from their fragments.

This makes for an exciting opportunity to combine the power of pLMs, agentic AI, and graph-assembly
techniques to investigate whether this combination of technology is effective in
guiding an accurate de novo protein reconstruction.

\subsection{Datasets}
As a source of data for experimentation and testing, UniProt Reviewed (Swiss-Prot) \cite{10.1093/nar/gkae1010}
served as an excellent resource for well-curated proteins. To condense the scope of the project
into something feasible, we decided to focus on only two organisms, Escherichia coli (E. coli) and Saccharomyces cerevisiae (yeast)
as they are well-studied and have a substantial amount of data publicly available on them.

Filtering the UniProt curated dataset left us with ~23,000 e. coli molecules and
~8,000 records for yeast. After filtering we fragment each molecule into appropriate
peptides according to trypsin digestion; an enzymatic process in which a trypsin protease
cuts the molecule into small peptide fragments by cleaving after lysine (K)
or arginine(R) residues, unless they are followed by proline(P). To simulate
a more realistic environement, we assume that 30\% of the time, the molecule
will not be cut at a given target site; i.e. will have a missed cleavage.
